Trauma-focused therapy for PTSD in displaced persons

Forcibly displaced persons are necessarily subject to experiences which contribute to post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). One meta-analysis reported an unadjusted, weighted PTSD prevalence of 30.6% in displaced persons (Steel et al., 2009). More people are displaced than ever recorded before (UNHCR, 2016) and PTSD is described as ten times more likely in displaced persons than in the general population (Nosé et al., 2017). They may experience detention in a camp low in resources (Crumlish & O’Rourke, 2010) but even on arrival in a host country, displaced persons endure instability and stress including the uncertainty of transition from status of ‘asylum seeker’ to ‘refugee’, ie. attaining some form of ‘leave to remain’ (UNHCR, 2009).  

A diagnosis of PTSD in DSM-V involves once or repeatedly experiencing, witnessing or learning of a threat of or actual death, injury or sexual violation to oneself or a close friend/family member. The resulting emotional disturbance, social or work impairment must be serious enough to be clinical (American Psychiatric Association, 2013; Foa et al., 2016). The construct has been thoroughly investigated in this population (Minihan et al., 2018) and treatment of PTSD symptoms in refugees is consistent with subsequent increased agency in pursuing improved circumstances (Nickerson et al., 2011).        

Trauma-focused, symptom-focused and multi-modal treatments have been studied (Cusack et al., 2016; Nickerson et al., 2011). Understanding PTSD symptoms as a conditioned response to cues associated with the original trauma has led to therapies aiming at the extinction of such associations. Considered alongside the role of cognition, distorted memories and appraisals of thoughts, these features invite a cognitive behavioural approach (Nickerson et al., 2011). The National Institute for Care and Health Excellence (NICE) guidelines recommend trauma-focused approaches, including cognitive processing therapy (CPT) and narrative exposure therapy (NET) as first line treatment for PTSD as well as eye movement desensitisation and reprocessing (EMDR) (Cusack et al., 2016). Trauma-focused treatments have been found to be more effective than pharmacological treatments (Drozdek, 2015).

Displaced persons

However, treatment of displaced persons with PTSD involves unique circumstances and difficulties. Interventions may occur in the context of transient residence/detention camps or in the health care system of a host country. In a new country, asylum seekers’ and refugees’ access to support can be limited by language, literacy, geography, state resources or policy and varying cultural norms regarding discussion of mental health (El Maazouz Alaoui & Scruby, 2019). People are more likely to initially present with somatic symptoms making screening and diagnosis a challenge (Century, Leavey & Payne, 2007).           

Once primary care has been accessed, it may be apparent that the experiences of displaced persons are alien to the practitioners of therapy and the interventions in their repertoire are designed for more acute, familiar issues but are being called upon in treating serious comorbid problems resulting from chronic severe abuse (Century, Leavey & Payne, 2007). Among displaced persons, PTSD issues are not discrete; the initial trauma is most likely not a single event; refugees will have current stressors in the new location, including stigma, policy regarding the status of refugees/asylum seekers and their access to resources (El Maazouz Alaoui & Scruby, 2019) and necessarily involving adjustment to new communities and pragmatic concerns such as housing and money (Hughes et al., 2019). Further, limitations to duration of intervention are a function of resource allocation and policy, not therapeutic need (Cowles & Griggs, 2019). The development of a therapeutic relationship can be compromised by the fact that clients have suffered violation of personal boundaries during their traumatic experience (Cowles & Griggs, 2019). On the other hand, some counsellors have noticed boundaries being crossed or removed when working with refugees/asylum seekers (Century, Leavey & Payne, 2007) leading Cowles and Griggs to recommend case-specific, critical assessment of the validity of boundary crossings (2019). All of which can lead to counsellors feeling overwhelmed, uncertain and powerless (Century, Leavey & Payne, 2007).

Key considerations in the therapeutic relationship with members of this population are: ‘acceptance, containment; awareness of past and present problems; empathy; lack of pressure; and witnessing’. However, the therapeutic relationship can also be compromised by: ‘limitation of resources, …third parties…, competing practical and psychological needs’ (p.27, Century, Leavey and Payne, 2007). Nickerson and colleagues also emphasise recognition of past and present stressors, prioritising current safety before treatment and devoting attention to immediate functional needs (2011). 

We will consider here CPT, NET and EMDR as the current recommendations of the relevant authority in Britain (Cusack et al., 2016).

Cognitive Processing Therapy

Whereas Beck’s CBT (1997) attended to present cognitions, Resick felt that PTSD required attention to past cognition. She adopted five key schemata from a constructivist theory of trauma: safety, trust, power, esteem and intimacy. Theoretically, ‘stuck points’ originate in trauma which is maladaptively integrated with these existing schemata, eg. by processing information in an altered way in constructing your reality (‘That wasn’t real rape. My home and husband still represent safety.’). A more adaptive approach would be to change an existing belief, ie. to ‘accommodate’. But some people ‘over-accommodate’ and acquire dysfunctional over-generalised beliefs. Making these stuck points and five key schemata the focus of an adapted CBT approach, Resick formulated a novel treatment for PTSD (Resick, Monson & Chard, 2016).     

The resulting techniques in CPT match features of CBT in general: identifying maladaptive beliefs, understanding emotional responses, restructuring inaccurate or overgeneralised beliefs and reducing avoidance as a coping strategy (Resick et al., 2008; Bolton et al., 2014).         

CPT allows for cultural adaptation (Kaysen et al., 2013) in spite of the fact concepts and terms (eg. esteem) may not have cross-cultural equivalents (Bernardi, Dahiya & Jobson, 2019). In Kurdistan, an adaptation had moderate effect size, it was deemed acceptable and can be successfully delivered by trained community mental health workers with little previous experience (Kaysen et al., 2013; Bolton et al., 2014) and, elsewhere, with no loss due to the use of interpreters (Schulz et al., 2006). The group format was seen to reduce feelings of isolation (Bernardi, Dahiya & Jobson, 2019) and CPT is seen to foster a sense of control and agency in one’s life (Nickerson et al., 2011).  

Among Karen refugees (Myanmar/Thailand), effects were less conclusive. Symptoms and diagnoses were lower post-treatment but at follow-up more than half of the small sample had worsened, suggesting sustained improvements may not result when service users remain exposed to continuing stressors (Bernardi, Dahiya & Jobson, 2019). Further to that, any instability of emotional reactions, suicidality or substance misuse must be resolved first (Nickerson et al., 2011).        

Although adaptation is possible, it is labour intensive and difficult to achieve. The process was iterative and required collaboration from locals with a background in health (Kaysen et al., 2013). In Kurdistan, no institutional provision existed for talking therapies, eg. private spaces, referrals from GPs, clinical supervision. Cultural/legal proscriptions hindered treatment, eg. opposite sex therapist/client dyads. And supervision by experienced practitioners was important but difficult to organise (Kaysen et al., 2013). Some cultural beliefs are in conflict with the principles of the treatment (negative events are the work of God) (p.188, Kaysen et al., 2013).         

Overall, CPT appears effective but requires sensitivity to context and logistics. In Massachusetts, where institutional support and resources existed for an impoverished and diverse population, CPT delivered with a flexible attitude towards the culture and beliefs of the service user has been qualitatively supported (Marques et al., 2016). Investigation of CPT’s compatibility with case formulation (individualising the therapy) is encouraging (Nixon & Bralo, 2019).

Narrative Exposure Therapy

Unlike CPT, NET was developed specifically to assist those who had experienced war, torture and political detention. It combines exposure therapy and testimony therapy (Neuner et al., 20020; Stenmark et al., 2013; Crumlish & O’Rourke, 2010). The therapist role is analogous to that in humanistic therapies in that empathy and lack of judgement are required (Bichescu et al., 2007) or in Rogers’ terms congruence and unconditional positive regard (Schauer, 2015). However, it shares the cognitivist understanding of PTSD (Bichescu et al., 2007). This is combined with the theory of ‘dual representation’ of traumatic memories. Cognitions, emotions and physiological responses (‘hot memory’, ‘associative memory’) are dissociated from episodic, contextual or verbally expressed elements (‘cold memory’, ‘autobiographic memory’) (Neuner et al., 2018). The result is that environmental or internal cues re-activate the physiological and emotional traumatic response (Neuner et al., 2018). The recurrence of this pattern causes avoidance to develop as a coping strategy (Schauer, 2015). Extinction of the habituated fear response is required (Mørkved et al., 2014).          

Treatment involves more adaptively integrating these elements of memory by associating emotional responses with a specific place and time in the narrative (Bichescu et al., 2007). Imaginal and in sensu exposure are achieved via discussion/re-experiencing of the whole lifeline and trauma, transcription and subsequent re-reading (Schauer, 2015). Finally, a written document of the biography is produced and acknowledged by all. Newly habituated emotional responses to the memory of trauma are believed to reduce PTSD symptoms (Bichescu et al., 2007; Stenmark et al., 2013).       

In comparison with non-specific psychotherapy, NET led to a noticeably larger reduction in PTSD symptoms (Stenmark et al., 2013). Meta-analyses and reviews found stronger support for NET’s efficacy than trauma-focused CBT (Crumlish & O’Rourke, 2010; Robjant & Fazel, 2010). Moderate and strong effect sizes were observed in Sri Lanka, Uganda, Somalia and from host countries with mixed samples (Gwozdziewycz & Mehl-Madrona, 2013). Nakeyar and Frewen considered NET the most supported intervention for this population (2016). Updating these results, insisting on quality research and reflecting on comorbidity and follow-up effects, Lely and colleagues remain convinced that NET is an effective treatment for PTSD in diverse displaced populations (2019).       

NET is applicable cross-culturally with little or no adaptation (Neuner et al., 2018), it exhibits a holistic and validating interest in the individual’s experience, likely absent previously and does not insist on ranking the significance of discrete traumas or events (Bichescu et al., 2007). Effects match theory: stress hormones impair the formation of context memory causing stimuli to prompt the ‘here and now’ appraisal of threat (Neuner et al., 2018); reduction in avoidance and arousal as symptoms is consistent with underlying mechanisms of habituation and reintegration (Bichescu et al., 2007). Compared to prolonged exposure, NET is better able to deal with complex and multiple trauma, which is more typical of refugees/asylum seekers (Mørkved et al., 2014). Non-professional delivery by peers is justified by the evidence, which facilitates wider dissemination of treatment (‘task-shifting’) (Neuner et al., 2008; Gwozdziewycz & Mehl-Madrona, 2013) and few resources are required for delivery (Mørkved et al., 2014). Finally, the biographical text acts as both a motivating outcome and serves political and social goals beyond the therapeutic context by supporting advocacy (Neuner et al., 2018).  

NET does have its limitations. As an exposure technique, NET risks adverse responses in people who remain subject to enormous burdens of stress (Nickerson et al., 2011). It is therefore also advisable to stabilise suicidal ideation and substance abuse before starting (Cusack et al., 2016). The logistics of delivery in camps can affect data gathering and interpretation of studies (Neuner, 2010). Effect sizes for those treated in their home regions were not different to those treated in Western host nations (Lely et al., 2019). This leads Lely and colleagues to suggest other modalities may have stronger effects among the resettled.   

However, overall, the support for NET is strong and relevant to multiple contexts.

Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing

Crumlish and O’Rourke, considering EMDR a successful treatment in the general population (Cusack et al., 2016), recommended it be studied in displaced persons too (2010). However, it remains much less investigated than CPT or NET. The aim of EMDR is to reduce the emotional arousal associated with a traumatic image by simultaneously holding the image in mind and engaging in saccadic eye movements. Theoretically, this induces an orienting response and an interference with working memory (Cusack et al., 2016).          

Four studies have been included in wider reviews and meta-analyses, two of which had large effect sizes in comparison to inactive controls in refugee camps (Acarturk et al., 2015; Acarturk et al., 2016) and two compared to stabilisation as usual with no significant differences (Ter Heide et al., 2011; Ter Heide et al., 2016). Cusack and colleagues’ meta-analysis reports evidence of efficacy for reduction of symptoms and loss of diagnosis of PTSD (2016). Since then, a significant effect has been found in a group of Syrian refugees, again against an inactive control (Yurtsever et al., 2018). To sum up, meta-analyses offer slight support for EMDR currently but encourage further investigation (Thomson, 2018; Turrini et al., 2018).    Concerns have also been raised that EMDR may not be appropriate with those suffering from psychosis, substance dependency or suicidal ideation (Ter Heide et al., 2016) and comorbid depression predicted poor treatment outcome in an RCT using EMDR as the intervention (Haagen et al., 2016). The current view of EMDR as an intervention for PTSD in displaced persons must, therefore, be that, it has some efficacy but little can be said relative to other interventions. It is safe, given the absence of other major mental health issues.

Conclusion and recommendations

No matter the modality of therapy offered, it is key that delivery is informed by the unique characteristics of this distressed population.            

In a host country, asylum seekers and refugees often have few or no relationships or resources comparable to that which they find in the primary care context. Counsellor training and supervision requires specialist input, including regarding management of boundaries. In such a context, therapy should form one part of a wider programme of assistance, addressing issues such as social support, acculturation, access to resources, work and accommodation (Lambert & Alhassoon, 2015). Both CPT and NET are appropriate and well-supported for this context, with the former likely to be more demanding for service providers.        

In camps, conflict zones or similar, ongoing trauma and recurring stressors must be considered as well as the culturally bound concepts and principles to be navigated. Adaptation of materials is resource and time demanding for CPT but again, both CPT and NET are well-supported here and both can be implemented via a ‘task shift’ dissemination of skills. However, NET is no doubt the easier method in which to train lay counsellors (Gwozdziewycz et al., 2013).     

Issues requiring more research include sustained effects and relapse in both contexts. We have also not considered here other approaches such as stress management, mindfulness and multi-modal approaches (Drozdek et al., 2015), or adolescents and children (Matulis et al., 2014). Minihan and colleagues comment fairly that a person-centred approach may be most appropriate due to the heterogeneity of people, trauma and symptoms in this population (2018). This is one more consideration along with logistical, contextual and cultural factors leading this paper to conclude that, of the three approaches discussed here, NET has the most to recommend it.
