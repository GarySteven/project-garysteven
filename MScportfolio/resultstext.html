Male and female preference for low-pitched voice

Results

We asked whether the preference for low-pitched voices was higher for male voices than female and whether listener sex was relevant. We hypothesised that, in the sample:  mean low pitch preference for male voices would be higher than mean low pitch preference for female voices and that mean low pitch preference would differ between male and female listeners (eg. male listeners preferring low-pitched voices to a different degree than female listeners do). We anticipated a possible interaction between listener sex and speaker sex (eg. male and female listeners feeling differently about female voices). The null hypotheses, therefore, are:  there is no difference in mean preference for male and female voices; there is no difference in male and female listeners’ mean preferences; and women and men react similarly to a change in the conditions.


 

 

The data appeared normally distributed within the conditions (see Figure 1) with close means and medians, generally acceptable skew and kurtosis (see Table 1), though in the male x male condition, kurtosis was ‘moderately non-normal’ (p.83, Blanca et al., 2013). Using boxplots and distance from the mean (1.5 times the inter-quartile range), seven outliers (female x female condition) were identified. Transformation of the outlying values to the group mean did not affect analysis. They were retained as initially recorded. Levene’s test for homogeneity of variance was not significant (p = .57). The data also retain observations from two conditions (direction of speech) which are not investigated (Binder et al., 2000).

Sample mean low pitch preference for female voices was similar for male listeners (Mmf = 53.74, SDmf = 16, n = 27) and female listeners (Mff = 53.87, SDff = 17, n = 33). When listening to male voices, both male mean preference (Mmm = 61.91, SDmm = 18, n = 27) and female mean preference for low pitch was higher (Mfm = 63.38, SDfm = 17, n = 33). In this condition, the female mean increased more (see Figure 2).

 

 

A mixed 2x2 ANOVA with listener sex as between-groups factor and speaker sex as within-groups factor revealed a significant speaker sex effect (Fspeak(1, 58) = 11.83, p < .001, ηp2 = .17). However, both the effect of listener sex (Flisten (1, 58) = 0.05, p = .82, ηp2 = 0.0009) and the interaction (Finter (1, 58) = 0.07, p = .8, ηp2 = 0.00113) were negligible and non-significant. Running a one sample, one-sided t-test power analysis on the within-subjects factor (a = .05, b = .2) shows the minimum detectable effect, Cohen’s dz = 0.33 (Lakens, 2013), is lower than the observed effect (dz = 0.6, 95% CI= [0.23]), suggesting sufficient power to identify the speaker sex effect. This accords with the ηg2 value (see Table 2) (Fritz et al., 2012). All calculated effect size measures suggest a medium to large effect of speaker sex and negligible effect of listener sex. No such analysis was considered necessary for the non-significant factor or interaction.

Results are consistent with our first hypothesis, and the effect size is medium to large though confidence interval and error bars are wide. Null hypotheses re the effect of listener sex and interaction are retained. Preference for low-pitched voices is higher for male voices than female regardless of listener sex.
