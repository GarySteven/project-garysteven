A Thematic Analysis of Adults’ Experience of Social Media Effects on Sleep

Introduction

To the many things that keep us up at night, we have added online social media and associated devices, the advent of which is still within memory. Among the negative consequences of online social media’s overwhelming prevalence, research has identified effects on sleep. Recent questionnaire studies have found shorter sleep duration, poorer sleep quality and sleep disturbance which are correlated with the specifics of self-reported use (Toms et al., 2017; Garett et al., 2016; Sampasa-Kanyinga et al., 2017; Levenson et al., 2017). Fossum et al. studied the use of electronic devices and found a positive relation between mobile phone use in bed and both insomnia and late chronotype (2010).

Other studies show: the suppression of melatonin by light-emitting electronic devices increases the risk of sleep disorder and insomnia (Chang et al., 2015); effortful control (albeit in infants) is correlated with quantity of sleep and general tablet use (Nathanson & Beyens, 2018); interruptions to routine by social media use are associated with increased likelihood of continual tiredness (Power, 2017) and the reason social media is allowed to interrupt sleep is often the “fear of missing out” (FOMO) (Scott, Woods & Biello, 2017).

Quantitative and qualitative methods have been applied. Quantitative approaches have demonstrated correlations, as described above, between self-reported behaviour and measures such as duration of sleep, academic success and 'eveningness'. However, as Fossum et al. point out, the results are inconsistent, particularly due to a lack of standardised operationalisation of concepts (2010). Self-report questionnaires also limit participants’ contributions to that which is explicitly sought by researchers, potentially missing relevant information. Scott, Woods and Biello instead took a qualitative approach, using a focus group to look at motivations and experience in more detail, finding that social expectations as well as fear of missing out influenced engagement with social media and sleep (2017).

While existing research has demonstrated a connection between social media use and effects on sleep, it has primarily been in samples of children, adolescents and young adults. An older cohort is better placed to comment on experience before and after inception of online social media. Also, Polos and colleagues found that effects on sleep and day-time tiredness associated with device use in bed changed with age (Polos et al., 2015). The current study was interested in exploring the experience of those who adopted social media as adults. As this transitional cohort is unique, it might be ill-advised to limit the data gathered. A qualitative approach in general and the focus group in particular are better able to generate data with fuller detail in responses with higher ecological validity, elucidating participants’ understanding of their own experiences (Willig, 2008). Therefore, this method was selected for our investigation.

            Data was collected on the 26th of May 2018 via a focus group conducted using the online video conferencing website ‘Zoom’, which allowed participants to see and hear each other in real time. The University of Glasgow School of Psychology’s ethical approval form was completed and all participants, aware of the aims of the project, completed an online consent form. This permitted the recording and transcription of the conversation (Appendix B), which was done using pseudonyms to maintain anonymity. All recordings and transcripts were stored on a password-protected computer and will be destroyed upon completion of the project.

            The research questions were formulated after a review of recent existing literature (see above). A focus group schedule was prepared in support of the main research questions:

·      What are the experiences of those who adopted social media as adults, especially with regards to effects on sleep?

·      How do they view their own use (optimal or not) and its consequences?

The full schedule can be seen in Appendix A, including the discussion points and prompts based on specific behaviour addressed in previous studies.

            The data were subjected to an inductive thematic analysis, though initial coding based on anticipated conceptual themes (reasons for use, effects on sleep, behaviour/use) was done after verbatim transcription. After initial coding, recurring comments and similarities of expressed belief or described behaviour were identified. Braun and Clarke describe the flexibility of this approach, “a method that works both to reflect reality and to unpick or unravel the surface of ‘reality’.” (p.81, 2006). The following analysis includes attempts to get beneath the surface of participants’ contributions into latent themes which relate to the research questions.

           

Analysis

Participants shared similar experiences and similar strong stances on optimal use of social media, which led to three key themes being identified in the thematic analysis. A measured resistance to the encroachment of social media was common to all participants, though their comments suggest this avowed resistance was often overcome, especially by content involving strong emotion, and sleep was affected. The tension between these facts might find resolution in a rationale by which social media use and its effects are presented as consciously integrated into a participant’s life. Thus, the themes considered below are (a) resistance as a desirable trait, (b) resistance overcome by emotional content, affecting sleep and (c) an integrative rationale for behaviour.

1. Resistance as a desirable trait

            All participants explicitly expressed their desire to limit social media’s intrusion on the hours of sleep, with the language used suggesting defiance, effortful control and sometimes direct contrast to the behaviour of others. The first extract to suggest resistance was John’s clear principle regarding the time immediately before bed: 

 

            ‘I don’t particularly use social media during those 30 minutes. I try to be technology-        free’ (John: 29-30)

 

The fact that John chooses ‘technology-free’ as something worth aspiring to ‘be’, the use of an adjective suggests that technology use is a manifestation of identity in his eyes. The clear adoption of a stance is seen further in Kate’s:

 

            ‘I'm like John I put my phone on airplane mode. It has to wait. Till the morning.’

            (Kate: 140-141)         

 

and also in Susan’s:

 

            ‘I completely agree with John on work messages. I will not...’ (Susan: 197).

 

Kate seems to also see this position as a matter of identity and both Kate and Susan choose their words carefully to indicate firm, stable opinions (Susan uses the uncontracted form, ‘I will not…’), indicating that this is even a matter of pride to some degree, a desirable characteristic.

            This desirable characteristic can be understood as existing in binary opposition to a perceived tendency among the wider population:

 

            ‘it's not that I have the need to check my phone’ (John: 109).

 

            For more examples of the resistance theme, see Appendix C.

 

2. Resistance overcome by emotional content, affecting sleep

            All participants, in spite of their resistance, described responding to social media, even when the notification is perceived as unwelcome and intrusive. These events constitute an interruption of routine and sleep habits.

            John, having previously denied ‘the need to check’ (John: 109), imagines himself in a passive role relative to external agencies (no suggestion of turning off the device is made) when he admits that his resistance can be overcome:

 

            ‘these geniuses of technology have come up with notifications even though you don't        want to check them you're going to see them and you're just going to fall for them’       (John: 113-115).

 

And the others, too, give concrete accounts of instances where they ‘fall for’ the notifications or the pull of social media (Susan: 143-144; Kate: 4-5).

Where resistance is overcome, one clear reason is invoked by the group. The consensus is that if sleep is affected or interrupted by social media it is as a result of particularly emotional content. This may be related to family, as Susan says:

 

‘if they haven't sent a message it's okay and if they have and they need something then     I'm there. It's difficult being far away sometimes’ (Susan: 124-126).

 

and in response to a niece’s ‘unfortunate’ messages:

 

            ‘I wake up with them it’s usually I'll check and then go back to sleep if it's nothing. I         mean without going into long details it's a long way away so’ (Susan: 144-146).

 

Or, it may be related to other content such as events in the news, as John says:

 

            ‘I don't perceive that it affects my sleep unless it is something politically that I find           annoying… any sort of event ... some sort of content that I find disturbing.’ (John: 31-35).

 

It should be acknowledged that John also points out that this is not necessarily a frequent occurrence and some of the instances presented answer a question regarding ‘caveats’ to the overall sense of resistance.

The issues which are cited as potentially disturbing sleep are decidedly adult concerns (parenthood, politics) and as such offer harder to refute arguments justifying social media’s intrusion on sleep. Which brings us to our final theme.

 

3. An integrative rationale for behaviour

Without exception, the participants declared a resistance to social media, while also accepting that it was likely to affect their sleep in some circumstances. These circumstances are, however, perceived as understandable and justified by their nature as adult, meaningful concerns. In fact, all participants can be understood as having formed an explicit rationale for their social media use, a rationale which places it in the wider context of their lives as well as emphasising their own agency to varying degrees.

 

‘it's mainly because of the time difference it's a good time of the day to contact people      in North America and I have two sons … so it's a reasonable time at around 11  o’clock’ (Susan: 12-14).

 

Kate also discusses how social media is one among many elements of her life which place demands on her time:

 

‘I'm very likely to use it before I go to sleep in those last 30 minutes because most            of the time I don't get a chance to use my phone and that's where I use it the most… it's          my chance to you know see what happened during the day [unintelligible] what’s going    on in life, really’ (Kate: 1-2).

 

And they make conscious decisions about when it is and is not appropriate to succumb to notifications. Susan agrees with the others when she says:

 

there's no reason to be responding quickly to work messages and I mean as far as friends and family [unintelligible] the kids they’re not going to get annoyed at you   because you didn’t answer right away. (Susan: 199-201)

 

Social media use is presented not as neither a trivial pastime or entertainment but as a tool which is integrated with varying degrees of control into the range of priorities present in their busy lives. The participants clearly have a considered conception of their relationship with social media, a conception which justifies intrusion while preserving some sense of freedom from an undesirable compulsion.

  

Discussion

After noting a tendency for research on the effects of social media to focus on adolescent and younger adults, this project has asked: What are the experiences of those who adopted social media as adults, especially with regard to effects on sleep? And, how do they view their own use (optimal or not) and its consequences? The three themes identified (resistance as a desirable trait; resistance overcome by emotional content, affecting sleep; an integrative rationale for behaviour) suggest that this group’s use of social media is less than optimal on their own terms and does affect sleep. Considered in light of the literature, it is likely that there are also unacknowledged negative effects. By identifying content as a cause, this sub-optimal situation is nonetheless justified within a wider view of their lives.

The resistance expressed by these three participants is in stark contrast to the fear of missing out and social expectation of availability which 11 to 17-year-olds described (Scott, Woods & Biello, 2017). Our more mature participants are extremely unlikely to be sending tweets between 2am and 6am as sleep disturbed American undergraduates did (Garrett, Liu & Young, 2016). However, this does not prevent them from routinely checking social media in the thirty minutes before bed, as did 32% of a large sample of 19 to 32-year-old Americans among whom such behaviour was significantly correlated with sleep disturbance (Levenson et al., 2017). Levenson et al. show caution regarding causal direction (ie. social media use causes sleep disturbance or the sleep disturbed turn to social media), as should we.  

Participants’ judgement proved perceptive in the identification of emotional content as a factor likely to affect sleep patterns. Being alert and engaged at bed-time makes it harder to fall asleep. Harbard et al. found significant correlations between pre-bedtime online social media use, cognitive pre-sleep arousal measures and longer sleep onset latency in school age children (2015). Our data is consistent with a similar experience among adults and therefore suggests potential value in triangulation via similar quantitative methods with this age group. Similarly, we found awareness of the effects of blue light on sleep onset latency but little reaction to this. The implications are that adult adopters are not necessarily better placed to resist the temptations of social media. Of course, when comparing our participants to others in the literature, especially at a more physiological level, we would do well to remember that children and adolescents have still-developing brains.

Our data could encourage more awareness-raising among adults that online media induced sleep disturbance is not exclusively a young person’s problem. Given that even the mature and strongly resistant can ‘fall for’ late night notifications, it may be appropriate to seek change. The perceptions of social media and associated devices found here could form useful feedback to manufacturers, media companies or government regulatory agencies. Advocacy may focus on manufacturers’ or media providers’ settings, for example. “Smart defaults” as defined by Smith, Goldstein and Johnson could be applied to blue light of screens, notifications and ‘do not disturb’ modes to promote the healthier settings as the automatic rather than the active choice (2013).

Our participants confidently assert their view that excessive social media use is undesirable while acknowledging that it affects their sleep in similar ways to that found in the literature.

A more social constructionist analysis may explore the concepts of ‘ideal use’ or ‘ideal users’ as proxies for more fundamental features of identity (Walker, 2015). Social media use has been found to negatively correlate with the “need for cognition” (NFC) scale, a measure of enthusiasm for effortfully thoughtful pursuits (Zhong, Hardin & Sun, 2011). Of the big five traits, some are positively associated with social media use, but conscientiousness has an inverse relationship (Liu & Campbell, 2017). Some awareness of these tendencies may exist popularly and conceivably creates negative connotations with high social media engagement.

There are of course limitations to the focus group approach. Firstly, we have identified themes in a small, homogeneous group which may or may not transfer to other groups and provides no evidence of causality. The operationalisation of ‘social media use’ was broad to say the least, with the conversation at times veering towards more traditional communications or pastimes. As always, exploring similar questions with a larger, diverse sample of participants and more narrowly defined parameters could be informative. Secondly, in a focus group, the facilitator and the focus group schedule are not neutral or passive but influence the data that is generated. In the present study, the invitation towards comparison in the topic and in the opening questions (eg. ‘What kind of user we are…’) may have contributed to the staking out of identities. Participants have an existing relationship and therefore may have been concerned to present themselves in a particular light. In the analysis, the researcher’s own ambivalence toward social media use may have impeded the acknowledgement of dissenting extracts in the data. Also, any consensus found must be considered alongside the possibility of conformity effects which can occur in group discussions (Acocella, 2012). It should be noted that one participant spoke assertively and at length, contributing significantly to data that suggested the three themes.

The influence of the literature and the research questions may have blurred the boundary between inductive and deductive analysis for this researcher. Nonetheless, the focus group approach and inductive analysis of latent themes, such as it was, would appear to have been successful in this case. The real value of qualitative research lies in finding the story behind the facts (Braun & Clarke, 2006). The main contribution made by this project is in bringing to light the slight conflict between what people say about social media use and their actions. We have investigated both the tendency of more mature adults to accept online social media’s encroachment into the hours of sleep and also how they reconcile this with a self-image as a conscious agent resisting the temptation to which others are more susceptible.
