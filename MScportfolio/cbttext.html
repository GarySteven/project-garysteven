Aggravation and obfuscation: neoliberalism, CBT and depression

The argument is not new that the dominance of the Cognitive Behavioural Therapy model (CBT) is due less to its efficacy than to its natural fit within a neoliberal agenda (Guilfoyle, 2008). The status of CBT is attributed to the Improving Access to Psychological Therapies programme in the UK (Pickersgill, 2019), the ‘Matrix’ in Scotland (NES, 2015) and to similar policy in Canada (Teghtsoonian, 2009). Its development is in contrast to contemporary views of mental wellbeing which contextualise the individual: the biopsychosocial model, ecological systems theory and fundamental cause theory (Engel, 1977; Bronfenbrenner, 1994; Phelan, Link & Tehranifar, 2010). These perspectives, in reaction to a biomedical view, advocate attention to the reciprocal influences between person, environment and social structures (present to greater extent in Ellis) (Engel, 1977; Dryden, 1994). This paper will argue that the evidence base for classic ‘2nd wave’ CBT is over-stated and what renders it convenient to conservative interests is partly its neglect of the social and biological. ‘3rd wave’ therapies will not be discussed directly, though the more philosophical objections will still apply. We will end with brief comments on further raising awareness of this issue in the wider population.     

The issue is particularly relevant in the treatment of depression among low socio-economic communities, where this illness is disproportionately prevalent (NES, 2016). CBT’s synthesis of an intrapsychic focus with an apparent biomedicalised ontology of illness is consistent with the neglect of structural inequalities and instead emphasises individual responsibility (Pickersgill, 2019). The result may be government policy demanding that the least fortunate exhibit Stoic resignation to the very material circumstances which increase their risk of suffering depression.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

CBT arose out of Ellis’s Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy and Beck’s diathetic cognitive model of depression (Beck, 1997, 2008; Ruggiero et al., 2018). In this view, depression involves dysfunctional beliefs or schemata, which negatively bias the processing of information leading to distorted cognitions regarding the self, the world and the future (Beck, 2005). These cognitions are accessible, are mediated but can be changed. Individuals have the ability to change their dysfunctional thoughts in order to improve affect (Beck & Dozois, 2011). Beck sought an integration of theory, empirical support and practice (1997) and the narrow focus on the intrapersonal facilitates a positivist view of illness (Williams, 2015). CBT, with a move toward more standardisation than REBT, is well-suited to a culture of categorical diagnosis, measurable outcomes, a focus on recovery rather than subjective wellbeing and, famously, the use of the randomised controlled trial (Slade et al., 2014; Pickersgill, 2019). All of which increases its attractiveness to policymakers; the step from an ability to change your thoughts to a responsibility to do so comes easier to some.  

The evidence base, however, is less compelling than claimed, not totally integrated with theory and inflated relative to alternative treatments.

Efficacy

Internationally, the consensus recommending CBT for depression is not universal (Moriana, Gálvez-Lara & Corpas, 2017). A thoroughly studied psychological intervention, CBT has shown strong effect sizes have been seen on depressed mood but with wide variability in maintenance of effects and no effect on the Quality of Life measure (Moore, Carr & Hartnett, 2016). In other meta-analyses, CBT for depression has been reported as more effective than waiting list controls or placebos, equal to or slightly superior to alternative therapies and more successful when combined with pharmacotherapy, all with mixed quality of evidence and small effect sizes (Butler et al., 2006; Hofman et al., 2012). No difference was found to positive psychology interventions in reducing depressive symptoms (Chavez et al., 2017).

In the clinical evidence informing the NICE guidelines, the strongest effect sizes in favour of CBT are for comparisons to wait-list controls; when compared to anti-depressants and other modalities (psychodynamic therapy, interpersonal therapy and gestalt therapy) little difference is found (NICE, 2010; Baardseth et al., 2013).

High rates of attrition are reported and evidence quality is sometimes low (Moore, Carr & Hartnett, 2016; Embling et al., 2002). The NICE summary excludes studies with more than 50% dropout (NICE, 2010). Attrition is not necessarily an indicator of flawed results (Amico, 2009) but CBT’s efficacy is closely related to: pre-treatment attitude of the client (McLellan, Stapinski & Peters, 2018) and the therapist (Parker & Waller, 2017); to pessimism and sensitivity to reward (Lopez-Gomez et al., 2017); to internal or external coping strategies (Baker & Neimeyer, 2003); to adherence (Kazantzis, Whittington & Dattilio, 2010; Kazantzis et al., 2018); to therapist competence (Easden & Fletcher, 2018); and to co-morbidity (Rasmussen, 2018). Not all CBT research reports reasons for dropout or full intent-to-treat analysis (Amico, 2009; Schultz & Grimes, 2002; Rasmussen, 2018).

The goal here is not to argue that CBT is ineffective, simply that its dominance is not justified on the evidence of efficacy alone and that this limits the valid options available to service users (Guilfoyle, 2008; Barkham, Moller and Pybis, 2017). When patients participate in treatment selection, adherence and outcome may be improved (Loh et al., 2014). Depressed patients have expressed their difficulty with following a programme of CBT; homework tasks can be a distressing burden (Barnes et al., 2013). But progress may be made in the updated guidelines (Barkham, Moller and Pybis, 2017).

Mechanism

Gipps challenges (philosophically) the entire conception of stimulus, sensation, thought, emotion, and physiological state as separable elements in a causal chain (2013). The ‘dodo effect’, whereby a psychological intervention of any kind appears more important than the specific modality, is often raised (Rasmussen, 2018; Guilfoyle, 2008). Others question the underlying mechanisms of CBT, arguing that results may not be due to the unique characteristics of the treatment (Leder, 2017; Cristea et al., 2015; Moriana et al., 2017). More evidence could be offered to suggest specific results are due to specific features of the intervention (more below, see: Singh & Gotlib, 2018).

RCTs

Research may be distorted by a ‘feedback loop’ in which an intervention which can be studied in RCTs is studied (p.12, Pickersgill, 2019). Comparisons are often between  group or individual CBT and a successor within the CBT family, eg. acceptance and commitment therapy (Zettle, Rains & Hayes, 2011) or resilience therapy (Zamirinejad et al., 2014) as opposed to a theoretically distinct treatment. Loewenthal goes further, on the ground that psychotherapy does not permit ‘control’ of all variables, questioning the overall appropriateness of the RCT, as does Rasmussen (2010; 2018).

            The features of CBT which accommodate RCTs, its manner of delivery, standardisation, use of categorical diagnosis, measurement of outcomes, are features that resemble the biomedical approach which Engel and others reacted against (1977).

Biological

An early critic of the narrow focus on cognition (Coyne & Gotlib, 1983), Gotlib recently summarised features of depression investigated in neuroimaging studies. These are often consistent with Beck’s model and include: difficulties with inhibition of negative information; ruminative responses and the inability to re-appraise and regulate mood. Associations have been observed between depression and networks involved with reward learning (dopaminergic system), emotional responses and memory formation (amygdala) and attention allocation (anterior cingulate cortex), not to mention serotonin transporters. But the key point is that depression involves heterogeneous symptoms with accordingly heterogeneous substrates (Singh & Gotlib, 2018; Pizzagali et al., 2019), including heterogeneity at the level of genetic antecedents (Flint & Kendler, 2014).          

In contrast to the ‘dodo effect’, Gotlib is optimistic that neuroimaging can identify distinct biomarkers of dissociable treatment response and argues therefore that interventions could address distinct substrates of depression (Singh & Gotlib, 2018). An link between anhedonia, narrowed associative thinking and fMRI imaging could be such a therapeutic target (Harel et al., 2016).             

Such an approach, requiring considerable expertise, technology and economic resources is one alternative to the standardised and manualised CBT delivered by low-paid paraprofessionals, a blunt tool by comparison.

Social

Pickersgill rightly objects to Layard’s characterisation of depression as a social problem measured in economic cost but it is a social problem in its causes, symptoms and solutions (Pickersgill, 2019; Cruwys et al., 2014). Attention to systems ‘from the molecule… to the biosphere’, or from the person to micro-, meso-, exo-, macro- and chrono-systems (Engel, 1977; Bronfenbrenner, 1994) provides at the very least a useful heuristic and at best a means of achieving ‘dual focus’ via the interactions between the surrounding environment and the individual (p.431 , Eriksson, Ghazinour & Hammarström, 2018).          

And so it is with depression, a condition without borders, with heritable and neuroendocrine components (Murata et al., 2008; Flint & Kendler, 2014; Cowen & Browning, 2015; Pizzagalli et al., 2019), intergenerationally incubated in families (Gotlib, Joorman & Foland-Ross, 2013; Steele et al., 2016) fed by adverse childhood experiences, maltreatment and family dysfunction (van Veen et al., 2012; Comijs et al., 2013; Taillieu et al., 2016) before work, financial strain and debt (Freeman et al., 2016; Drentea & Reynolds, 2012) or a social loss (Cruwys et al., 2014) have their effects exacerbated by a lack of community (Domenech-Abella et al., 2018), interpersonal trust and reciprocity (Han et al., 2018). Low socio-economic status is well established as a risk factor for depression (Lorant et al., 2007; Butterworth, Rodgers & Winter, 2009; Meltzer et al., 2010; Gavin et al., 2010; Freeman et al., 2016).       

These biological and social contributors to depression reflect material circumstances, changes in which would constitute full attention to the issue. By characterising thought as pathological as opposed to legitimate ‘captured discontent’ (p.80, Fisher, 2009), CBT burdens the service user (Barnes et al., 2013) and exhorts people to change themselves rather than their circumstances, absolving government of its obligations. Rather than question prevailing power structures or culturally enforced concepts or status (eg. gender roles) (Guilfoyle, 2009) it participates in the prevailing discourse of ‘responsibilisation’ which makes a virtue of self-care. By implication, recourse to state support becomes a vice to be abjured by ‘good citizens’ (p.31, Teghtsoonian, 2009).

One explicitly stated aim of IAPT, matching the ‘recovery model’ of health care, was to reduce claims on incapacity benefit (Slade et al., 2014; Pickersgill, 2019). The rate of re-assessment of existing claimants via the Work Capability Assessment was linked to an increase in suicide, self-reported mental health issues and prescription of anti-depressants, which inevitably affected deprived areas disproportionately (Barr et al., 2016). 

It is difficult to believe that this is a result of naiveté, complacence or incompetence. Such policy has been rightly condemned as ‘a harsh and uncaring ethos’ (p.1, Alston, 2019). The UK Government, through cuts to social services and the use of CBT to responsibilise the individual, simultaneously aggravates and obfuscates the causes of depression in a dexterous act of legerdemain (Bambra, 2019; Main & Bradshaw, 2016).

Communication

Institutional social marketing for health is often regarded with suspicion (Ley, 1998; Crawshaw, 2012). However, communication of this paper’s position is necessarily subversive to some degree (House, 2012) and institutional channels are unlikely to match the message. The difficulty then is to educate, persuade or protest without engaging in what Foucault has called ‘governing at a distance’ (Crawshaw, 2012). When health care providers do not spend enough time with service users (Clark, Goodman & Petitti, 2018; Crawshaw, 2012), those users are more likely to seek out information on youtube (Langford & Loeb, 2019). In that forum, user-generated science communication is more popular than professional (Paek et al., 2011). As in other domains (Fiske & Dupree, 2014), trustworthiness was key among factors creating youtube source credibility that also included expertise, peer as source and likeability (Xiao, Wang & Chan-Olmsted, 2018). Content of argument predicted credibility but when looking at personally relevant topics, viewers used peripheral processing (Cacioppo & Petty, 1986; Arora, 2018), as depressed individuals also tend to do (Neimeyer, Metzler & Dongarra, 1990). Popular youtube creators form parasocial relationships via gaze, gesture and prosody (Frobenius, 2014), ‘breaking the fourth wall’, authenticity, having a face on screen and inclusive syntactics (Ferchaud et al., 2018).   

Such a medium may be appropriate for a message critical of the established recommendations. CBT may be suited to those whose experience is not as severe as others and who have an internal coping strategy. It may relieve symptoms. But it also facilitates the neglect of wider issues which involve contexts and groups rather than individuals. Much broader approaches, in fundamental cause theory (Phelan, Link & Tehranifar, 2010) and the practice of psychological formulation (Johnstone, 2018) may be preferable even to CBT’s later incarnations such as Mindfulness-based Cognitive Therapy or Acceptance and Commitment Therapy. These bear the same characteristic attention to thought over circumstances (Kahl, Winter & Schweiger, 2012).

Conclusion

Psychologists may not have the power to address the material circumstances of population groups but are capable of recognising their own participation in a revival of Epictetus’s Stoicism into a ‘neat little package that you could sell’ using the cachet of evidence-based practice (p.12, Rosner, 2012 in Rasmussen, 2018). This positivist synthesis of the psychological and the biomedical undermines a hard-won rights-based ethics in favour of a regressive ethics of virtue wherein the poor must take a cosmic view of their given place.

In the Stoic cosmos, suffering is an opportunity to demonstrate virtue but, in such a cosmos, the fortunate have no need of virtue.
